GENETIC AND GENOMIC FRONTIERS IN ARRHYTHMIA MANAGEMENT
Professional Presentation Summary
Prepared: 2026-05-08
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION — WHY GENETICS MATTERS IN ARRHYTHMIA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The inherited arrhythmia syndromes collectively represent a major — and preventable
— cause of sudden cardiac death (SCD) in the young. They affect an estimated 1 in
500 to 1 in 2,500 individuals depending on subtype, with many deaths occurring in
previously asymptomatic patients with structurally normal hearts.

A fundamental shift has occurred over the past two decades: from phenotype-first
management (treat the ECG or the cardiomyopathy) toward genotype-informed care
(predict risk, tailor therapy, and screen families before a sentinel event). This
shift is driven by:

  - The identification of over 100 disease-causing genes across channelopathies
    and cardiomyopathies
  - Commercially available multigene panel testing
  - Emerging gene-specific risk calculators
  - The first gene therapy trials specifically targeting inherited arrhythmias
  - Growing evidence that many "Mendelian" arrhythmias are partly polygenic

The clinical stakes are high: SCD may be the first manifestation of disease;
cascade family screening can identify asymptomatic relatives before tragedy;
and gene-specific therapies are now moving from bench to bedside.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: THE GENETIC ARCHITECTURE OF INHERITED ARRHYTHMIAS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

2A. CHANNELOPATHIES
-------------------

Channelopathies arise from gain-of-function (GOF) or loss-of-function (LOF)
mutations in cardiac ion channels, disrupting normal action potential generation
and propagation. The final common pathway is altered repolarization reserve —
often unmasked by triggers such as exercise, emotion, fever, drugs, or sleep.

LONG QT SYNDROME (LQTS)
  - Most prevalent channelopathy: ~1:2,500; possibly as high as 1:2,000
  - 17 genetic subtypes; 3 major forms account for ~75% of genotype-positive cases:
      LQT1 (KCNQ1, 30-35%): IKs LOF → exercise/swimming triggered events
      LQT2 (KCNH2, 25-30%): IKr LOF → auditory stimuli/postpartum triggered events
      LQT3 (SCN5A, 5-10%): INaLate GOF → rest/sleep triggered events
  - ClinGen 2020: only KCNQ1, KCNH2, SCN5A have definitive evidence for isolated
    LQTS; KCNE2 removed from the gene list
  - Overall variant detection rate: 70-80%
  - Asymptomatic variant carriers carry a 10-fold increased arrhythmic risk vs.
    non-carriers — justifying testing of all first-degree relatives
  - ESC 2022 diagnosis: QTc ≥480 ms on repeated ECG, Schwartz Score ≥3.5, OR
    pathogenic variant irrespective of QTc
  - Key risk modifier: sex hormones — oestradiol inhibits IKr (longer QT, higher
    female risk age 18-40); progesterone is antiarrhythmic (LQT2 postpartum risk)
  - Up to 40% of LQTS patients have QTc <450 ms at baseline — QTc alone insufficient

BRUGADA SYNDROME (BrS)
  - Prevalence: ~1:2,000 for type 1 ECG; ~80-90% of diagnosed patients are male
  - SCN5A LOF is the only ClinGen definitively causative gene — found in only ~30%
    of probands
  - GWAS has identified 21 independent loci (HEY2, MAPRE2 among novel ones),
    confirming a largely polygenic architecture for the remaining ~80%
  - Three competing, non-mutually exclusive hypotheses: depolarization (RVOT
    fibrosis/conduction delay), repolarization (Ito-driven phase 2 re-entry),
    and neural crest cell migration defects
  - Male predominance explained by KChIP2 upregulation via androgen receptor
    signalling → higher Ito density → greater propensity for AP dome loss
  - Risk stratification: spontaneous type 1 ECG + cardiogenic syncope carries
    2.3-3.7%/year SAE rate; asymptomatic with drug-induced type 1: 0.21-0.3%/year
  - Annual ICD complication rate (inappropriate shocks 3.3% + other 4.5%) exceeds
    SCD rate in asymptomatic patients — a significant management tension

CATECHOLAMINERGIC POLYMORPHIC VENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIA (CPVT)
  - Prevalence ~1:10,000; onset 7-12 years; up to 50% mortality by age 35 if
    unrecognized; frequently misdiagnosed as epilepsy
  - RYR2 GOF (CPVT1): ~50-60%; autosomal dominant
    Mechanism: SR Ca2+ overload → spontaneous Ca2+ sparks → NCX1 activation →
    delayed afterdepolarizations → triggered bidirectional VT
  - CASQ2 LOF (CPVT2): ~5%; autosomal recessive; more severe, earlier onset,
    greater beta-blocker resistance
  - Largest CPVT database (Chang 2025, Marks Lab): 964 patients, 263 unique RYR2
    variants; domain-specific onset (core solenoid/pore: median 8y; EF-hand: 28y)
  - Variant-specific beta-blocker efficacy: p.G2337V 91% effective; p.C2277R 25%
  - First in-vivo CRISPR RYR2 repair (Pan 2023): 41% efficiency, 0/7 treated vs.
    7/8 untreated mice had arrhythmias; no off-target edits
  - CASQ2 overexpression also suppressed CPVT1 model arrhythmias — pathway-directed
    approach to bypass the ~15,000 nt RYR2 AAV packaging barrier

OTHER CHANNELOPATHIES
  - Short QT Syndrome: QTc ≤360 ms; 8 subtypes (KCNH2 most common); quinidine
    first-line pharmacotherapy
  - Early Repolarization Syndrome: J-point elevation in VF survivors; mechanistic
    partner of Brugada syndrome (Ito-mediated)
  - Andersen-Tawil Syndrome (LQT7): KCNJ2 LOF triad — arrhythmia + periodic
    paralysis + dysmorphic features
  - Idiopathic VF: diagnosis of exclusion after VF; short-coupled R-on-T PVCs


2B. ARRHYTHMOGENIC CARDIOMYOPATHIES
-------------------------------------

Unlike channelopathies, these conditions involve structural myocardial disease —
but arrhythmia is often the first and most dangerous manifestation, preceding
heart failure by years or decades.

ARVC / ARRHYTHMOGENIC CARDIOMYOPATHY (ACM)
  - ACM is an umbrella encompassing ARVC (RV-dominant), ALVC (LV-dominant),
    and biventricular forms
  - Core causative genes span multiple protein classes:
      Desmosomal: PKP2 (~2/3 of ARVC), DSP (ALVC archetype), DSG2, DSC2, JUP
      Nuclear envelope: LMNA (highest malignant VA risk of all CM genes)
      Calcium handling: PLN (R14del founder mutation; exercise NOT a risk factor —
        unique exception among ACM genes)
      Transmembrane: TMEM43 (p.Ser358Leu; Newfoundland founder; highly malignant)
      Cytoskeletal: FLNC (truncating variants; 82% SCD/VA rate)
  - Autosomal dominant predominates; 3-6% carry multiple pathogenic variants →
    earlier onset (<20 vs. 35 years) + higher lifetime SCD risk
  - Penetrance is incomplete and age-related; highest between 20-50 years
  - The "final common pathway" hypothesis: all ACM subtypes converge on intercalated
    disc dysfunction — the desmosome in ARVC; the area composita more broadly
  - Cascade family screening: every 1-3 years from age 10-12 in first-degree relatives
  - Nomenclature conflict: HRS 2019 embraces ACM umbrella; ESC 2023 rejects it,
    preferring ARVC (RV) and NDLVC (LV non-dilated with non-ischaemic scar)

HCM AS ARRHYTHMIA SUBSTRATE
  - Most common inherited cardiomyopathy; MYBPC3 and MYH7 account for ~80% of
    solved cases (combined yield 30-60%)
  - Sarcomeric variants (MYBPC3/MYH7) drive primary LA fibrosis → atrial myopathy
    → AF risk independent of obstruction
  - Pre-structural ion channel remodelling: INaL/ILTCC changes detectable in HCM
    before phenotypic hypertrophy — electrophysiological risk precedes anatomy
  - HCM Risk-SCD calculator: 5-year SCD risk model; validated for primary prevention
    ICD decisions; genotype does not yet enter the calculator directly

DCM WITH ARRHYTHMIC GENES
  - TTN (titin) is the most common DCM gene (TTNtv 10-20%); also the most common
    early-onset AF gene (27% of variants)
  - LMNA: 5.5% of DCM; AF and conduction disease precede ventricular cardiomyopathy
    by years; gene-specific ICD consideration before LVEF falls to 35%
  - SCN5A LOF: overlap syndrome — BrS + conduction disease + DCM from same variants
  - Gene-specific risk calculators exist for VA risk in DCM: LMNA-risk-VTA
    (lmna-risk-vta.fr); LGE + genotype + LVEF are the key multi-parameter inputs


2C. EARLY-ONSET ATRIAL FIBRILLATION
--------------------------------------

Early-onset AF (EOAF) is defined operationally as AF before age 66. It is far more
likely to have a monogenic cause than AF in older patients — and increasingly
recognized as an arrhythmia-first manifestation of inherited cardiomyopathy.

GENETIC YIELD
  - In 1,293 prospective EOAF patients (whole-genome sequencing, 145 gene panel):
    10.1% carried a pathogenic/likely pathogenic (P/LP) variant — comparable to
    non-familial DCM (~10%) and far exceeding the ~2% general population rate
  - Age gradient: 16.8% yield for AF <30 years; 7.1% for ages 60-65
  - Predominantly cardiomyopathy genes — not channelopathies:
      DCM genes:  7.2%   (TTN most common)
      HCM genes:  3.3%   (MYBPC3, MYH7)
      AC/ARVC:    2.9%   (PKP2, DSP)
      LQTS:       0.9%
      Brugada:    0.2%
  - 62.8% of patients carry a VUS as their highest-priority finding — the most
    challenging clinical scenario with no standardized management pathway

UK BIOBANK POPULATION-SCALE DATA (n=403,990 WES, JAMA 2024)
  - Six pLOF genes associated with AF in gene-burden testing:
    TTN, RPL3L, PKP2, CTNNA3, KDM5B, C10orf71 (5/6 externally replicated)
  - TTN (PSI90 exons) strongest association: OR 3.85 for incident AF
  - Combined PRS + pLOF variant model: OR 7.08 (95% CI 6.03-8.28) for AF;
    10-year absolute risk 24% in males >60 with top PRS + pLOF variant
  - Novel genes: KDM5B (histone H3K4 demethylase) and RPL3L (ribosomal function)
    implicate epigenetic and translational mechanisms beyond structural proteins

POLYGENIC × MONOGENIC INTERACTION
  - TTNtv carriers in the highest PRS tertile: 21.5% AF prevalence
    TTNtv carriers in the lowest PRS tertile: 6.7% AF prevalence
    — >3-fold range in penetrance explained by common variant background
  - Same interaction confirmed in HCM: P/LP carriers in highest PRS centile have
    14-fold increased risk vs. median PRS; OR 9.56 vs. 3.69

ATRIAL MYOPATHY HYPOTHESIS
  - EOAF often represents an "atrial myopathy" — genetic defects in sarcomeric,
    cytoskeletal, or nuclear envelope proteins causing atrial structural dysfunction
    before overt ventricular cardiomyopathy becomes detectable
  - MYH6 (alpha-myosin heavy chain; atrial-predominant expression) may selectively
    manifest as AF without ventricular involvement
  - AF precedes ventricular cardiomyopathy in ~50% of TTNtv cases who develop both
    — challenging the assumption that AF is purely downstream of established disease
  - LMNA: isolated AF as the sole initial presentation, preceding structural
    cardiomyopathy by years to decades

CLINICAL PHENOTYPE: VERY YOUNG AF (<40 years WITHOUT SHD)
  - Largest prospective cohort (n=122; mean age 31.2y; 83% male; follow-up 52 mo):
      89% had ≥1 lifestyle risk factor
      25% had hereditary predisposition (familial AF 16%; FHx CVD 10%)
  - Genetic yield in this phenotype: 11.6% P/LP (TTN x4, LMNA x2, PKP2 x2, TNNI3 x1)
  - Structural disease absent at presentation does NOT rule out cardiomyopathy
    genotype — CMR was normal in initial assessments but 5/13 P/LP carriers later
    showed ventricular enlargement on follow-up CMR
  - Stroke in 4.1% despite CHA2DS2-VA = 0 — all unticoagulated at time of event;
    conventional risk models may underestimate thromboembolic risk in very young AF
  - Catheter ablation outcomes excellent: 86% success at 1 year; 93% after 2 procedures

GUIDELINE POSITION
  - 2023 ACC/AHA AF guidelines (Class IIb): genetic testing reasonable in patients
    with AF diagnosed at ≤45 years with no obvious risk factors — the first formal
    guideline recommendation for genetic testing specifically in AF
  - ESC AF guidelines (current): NO recommendation for genetic testing in young
    individuals without comorbidities — a notable gap vs. ACC/AHA
  - Comparator inconsistency: genetic testing is Class IB in ESC guidelines for DCM;
    diagnostic yield in non-familial DCM (~10%) equals EOAF — making absence of
    formal EOAF recommendations a notable inconsistency
  - Clinical implication: EOAF patients benefit from evaluation in an inherited heart
    disease clinic; identifying a cardiomyopathy variant triggers CMR surveillance,
    echocardiography, and cascade family screening even with a normal initial echo

SECTION 3: FROM MONOGENIC TO POLYGENIC — THE FULL SPECTRUM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inherited arrhythmia syndromes are not purely Mendelian. GWAS has established that
common polygenic variants contribute substantially to susceptibility and expressivity
across channelopathies and cardiomyopathies.

KEY GWAS FINDINGS BY CONDITION

  BrS:  21 independent GWAS loci; SCN5A explains only ~20% of cases; novel loci
        HEY2 (RVOT transcription factor) and MAPRE2 (cytoskeletal) implicate
        developmental and structural mechanisms beyond ion channels
  LQTS: Common SNPs explain ~15% of susceptibility variance; 176 independent
        QT-SNPs identified (2022, n=252,977 multiancestry GWAS)
  HCM:  PRS predicts case status OR 2.34/SD; inverse MYBPC3/ALPK3/FHOD3 loci
        confirm hypercontractility (HCM) vs hypocontractility (DCM) axis
  DCM:  PRS OR 1.73/SD; validated across European, African, and Admixed-American
        cohorts (Jurgens 2024) — unusually strong multi-ancestry portability
  AF:   Combined PRS + pLOF variant → OR 7.08 for AF; top-quintile PRS alone ~OR 3.5

MODIFIER GENES
  - NOS1AP: most validated LQTS modifier; risk alleles associated with life-
    threatening arrhythmia in LQTS independent of primary mutation; mechanistically
    confirmed via NOS1 activity regulation in hiPSC-CMs and mouse models
  - KCNH2-K897T: converts latent LQT2 to symptomatic disease; associated with
    post-AMI life-threatening arrhythmias
  - KCNE1-D85N: sex-specific modifier — prolongs QTc in male but not female LQT1
    carriers; strong recommendation to avoid IKr-blocking drugs even without formal
    diagnosis

POLYGENIC RISK SCORES (PRS)
  - QT-PRS: top decile QTc ~8.7 ms longer than population mean — comparable to
    some common monogenic LQTS variants in effect size
  - Drug-induced LQTS PRS: explains ~30% of QTc variance during drug exposure
    (OR 1.34/SD) — common variant architecture dominates pharmacogenomic QT response
  - BrS PRS: OR 2.12/SD for spontaneous type 1 ECG and positive SCB provocation;
    CRITICAL LIMITATION — does NOT predict lethal arrhythmic events (VF/SCD)
  - 75% of individuals with markedly prolonged QTc lack both high PRS AND identifiable
    rare pathogenic variant — drugs, electrolytes, and autonomic tone dominate

CLINICAL IMPLICATION
  - Genotype-negative patients are not necessarily "non-genetic" — they may carry
    high polygenic burden without a single dominant rare variant
  - PRS and rare variant testing are complementary tools; no validated algorithm
    currently combines them into a single actionable clinical risk estimate
  - EOAF: AF PRS does NOT predict cardiomyopathy (HR 0.99), but pLOF variants do
    (CM HR 3.13 before AF; HR 2.98 after AF) — confirming distinct mechanisms

SECTION 4: GENETIC TESTING IN CLINICAL PRACTICE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PRINCIPLES AND INDICATIONS
  - Multigene panel testing is the current standard of care — superior to serial
    single-gene testing due to genetic heterogeneity and cost efficiency
  - Next-generation sequencing (NGS) allows simultaneous analysis of dozens of
    disease-relevant genes; cost continues to decline
  - Combined cardiomyopathy + arrhythmia panels (up to 150 genes) outperform
    disease-specific panels: 10.9% additional diagnoses in single-indication patients;
    36% of gained diagnoses were cross-category (arrhythmia → cardiomyopathy gene)
  - Proband testing first: start with the most clearly affected, most severely
    affected family member to maximise yield

DIAGNOSTIC YIELD BY PHENOTYPE

  Condition                  Yield       Key Genes
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  HCM (age 19-39)            40.4%       MYBPC3, MYH7 (~80% of solved)
  HCM (all ages)             25-60%      MYBPC3, MYH7, TNNT2, TNNI3
  DCM (familial)             10-40%      TTN (10-20%), LMNA (5.5%), RBM20
  ARVC (Task Force+)         up to 63%   PKP2, DSP, DSG2, DSC2, JUP
  LQTS                       26.1%       KCNQ1, KCNH2, SCN5A
  Brugada Syndrome           14.9%       SCN5A only (ClinGen definitive)
  Early-onset AF             ~10%        TTN, LMNA, PKP2, TNNI3
  CPVT                       ~4%         RYR2, CASQ2

EARLY-ONSET AF TESTING SPECIFICS
  - 2023 ACC/AHA: genetic testing Class IIb for AF ≤45 years without obvious
    risk factors; Class IIb EP study for AF <30 years to exclude SVT
  - Combined cardiomyopathy/arrhythmia panel recommended — not channelopathy-only
  - 5% of DCM patients harbour both ion channel and cardiomyopathy gene variants;
    10% of EOAF patients carry cardiomyopathy gene variants — panels must cross-cover
  - TTN truncating variant (TTNtv) classification challenge: found in 10-20% of DCM
    but also ~1% of general population and athletes; may function as a risk allele
    requiring a "second hit" rather than fully penetrant pathogen

VARIANT CLASSIFICATION CHALLENGES
  - ACMG/AMP 5-tier classification (Pathogenic → Benign) is the universal standard
  - Overall reclassification rate >36% in inherited cardiovascular diseases;
    cardiac channelopathies: 38%; cardiomyopathies: 28%
  - The majority of reclassifications are DOWNGRADES (P/LP → VUS) driven by updated
    population database frequencies and reassessment against current ACMG criteria
  - Annual LP/P reclassification rate: 1-8%/year; VUS reclassify at even higher rates
  - HCM: 11% of variants reclassified over 6 years — re-evaluate every 2-3 years
    (Class I, AHA 2024)
  - No standardized infrastructure exists for systematic family notification when
    reclassification occurs — families managed under a now-incorrect genetic diagnosis

VUS MANAGEMENT
  - VUS found in 51.2% of all patients tested with broad panels; 62.8% of EOAF
    patients; higher in non-European populations (underrepresentation in databases)
  - VUS CANNOT drive cascade genetic testing — phenotypic family evaluation instead
  - Segregation analysis (co-segregation with phenotype across affected relatives) is
    the most powerful tool for VUS reclassification
  - Re-evaluate every 1-3 years; each follow-up should re-interpret against updated
    ClinVar/ClinGen databases

DTC GENETIC TESTING CONSIDERATIONS
  - DTC-GT uses SNP-chip genotyping in most cases, NOT full sequencing — substantially
    higher false-negative rate than clinical-grade panels
  - Confirmatory CLIA-certified testing mandatory before any management change
  - A negative DTC result does NOT exclude a genetic cardiomyopathy or channelopathy

CASCADE FAMILY SCREENING
  - Once P/LP variant identified in proband: cascade genetic testing of all at-risk
    first-degree relatives
  - Genotype-negative relative: substantially reduced risk; can be discharged from
    serial phenotype surveillance (with instruction to return if symptoms develop)
  - Genotype-positive, phenotype-negative: serial phenotypic surveillance at intervals
  - ACM families: cascade screening every 1-3 years from age 10-12
  - EOAF: CMR-based longitudinal surveillance even when initial echo is normal

SECTION 5: GENOTYPE-PHENOTYPE CORRELATIONS — PRECISION RISK STRATIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Genetic subtype determines not just risk level but which specific triggers,
therapies, and surveillance protocols are appropriate. A one-size-fits-all
approach is no longer acceptable. The Mayo Clinic 20-year specialist cohort
(n=1,304 LQTS patients) demonstrated that expert management requires up to
18 distinct therapeutic configurations for a single genetic subtype (LQT3)
alone — a level of precision that is fundamentally incompatible with
algorithmic non-specialist prescribing.


LQTS — GENE-SPECIFIC CLINICAL MANAGEMENT
=========================================

TRIGGER PATTERNS AND ECG SIGNATURES (FIRST-LINE RECOGNITION)

  LQT1 (KCNQ1):  Exercise and swimming are primary triggers. IKs is nearly
                  absent at resting heart rates — the phenotype is unmasked by
                  exercise (QTc paradoxically fails to shorten or widens on
                  stress testing). ECG: broad-based smooth T-wave. Beta-blockers
                  are maximally effective in LQT1 — the most responsive subtype.

  LQT2 (KCNH2):  Auditory stimuli (alarm clocks, doorbells, telephone ringing)
                  and emotional arousal are hallmark triggers. Postpartum is the
                  highest-risk period (2.7-fold cardiac event risk; 4.1-fold
                  life-threatening event risk vs. prepregnancy baseline in the
                  9 months after delivery). ECG: bifid (notched) T-wave.
                  Transmembrane pore variants carry the highest relative risk
                  within LQT2.

  LQT3 (SCN5A):  Arrhythmic events occur predominantly at rest and during sleep
                  (bradycardia-dependent QT prolongation). First events in LQT3
                  are more likely to be immediately fatal compared to LQT1/2.
                  ECG: delayed T-wave onset with long isoelectric ST segment.
                  Onset typically post-puberty.

LQT1 — TREATMENT HIERARCHY IN CLINICAL PRACTICE

  First line:     Non-selective beta-blockers (nadolol preferred; once-daily,
                  superior compliance data). Beta-blockers are more protective
                  in LQT1 than in any other LQTS subtype — exercise is the
                  dominant trigger and BB directly suppresses the adrenergic
                  stimulus.
  Escalation:     LCSD (left cardiac sympathetic denervation) for BB intolerance
                  or breakthrough events on adequate BB therapy.
  Monotherapy:    LCSD was used as sole therapy in 5% of the Mayo Clinic LQTS
                  cohort (42 LQT1 + 18 LQT2 patients); BB intolerance was the
                  primary indication in 90%. BCE (breakthrough cardiac event)
                  rate was 5% (all non-lethal); zero lethal events at follow-up.
  Sports:         LQT1 competitive sports participation is reasonable with shared
                  decision-making under expert supervision — zero deaths in the
                  largest LQTS sports cohort (n=494 athletes). LQT1 swimming
                  can be considered with CPR-trained supervision and AED on
                  site; most prior SCA cases were in undiagnosed/untreated
                  patients.

LQT2 — TREATMENT HIERARCHY IN CLINICAL PRACTICE

  First line:     Non-selective beta-blockers + avoidance of auditory triggers
                  (smartphone notifications on silent mode, graduated alarm
                  sounds, minimise night-time disturbances).
  Postpartum:     Class I — continue beta-blockers throughout pregnancy and for
                  ≥40 weeks postpartum. Postpartum cardiac event rate: 3.7% →
                  0.8% with beta-blocker use. Risk reverts to baseline after
                  9 months postpartum.
  High-risk LQT2 — IPAP (intentional permanent atrial pacing):
                  Atrial pacing at ≥80 bpm shortens QTc at the paced rate and
                  suppresses pause-dependent TdP initiation. In 35 high-risk
                  LQT2 patients (mean QTc 501 ms), IPAP reduced breakthrough
                  cardiac event rate from 1.01 to 0.02 per patient-year
                  (p=0.003). An atrial pacemaker WITHOUT defibrillation capability
                  may be sufficient for select high-risk LQT2 — an important
                  distinction that avoids ICD complications in a subset.
  Refractory LQT2 — mexiletine (off-label, INaLate-targeted):
                  Although guideline-directed therapy restricts mexiletine to
                  LQT3, the Mayo Clinic experience in a high-risk LQT2 subset
                  (mean baseline QTc 543 ms, 47% with prior breakthrough events)
                  showed mexiletine reduced QTc by a mean of 65±45 ms and
                  achieved ZERO breakthrough cardiac events over 6.5 years of
                  follow-up. This challenges strict genotype-to-drug assignment:
                  when phenotypic severity is extreme (QTc >540 ms), late sodium
                  current likely plays a clinically meaningful role even in LQT2.
  Device choice:  SQ-ICD is RARELY used in LQT2 due to T-wave oversensing
                  (bifid T-wave morphology → inappropriate sensing and shocks).
                  Transvenous ICD or pacemaker/ICD preferred.

LQT3 — TREATMENT HIERARCHY IN CLINICAL PRACTICE

  The most therapeutically complex LQTS subtype. Up to 18 distinct treatment
  configurations were identified in the Mayo Clinic LQT3 cohort.

  Beta-blocker choice:
                  Propranolol is preferred over nadolol in LQT3. Propranolol
                  directly blocks the late sodium current (INaLate) — the primary
                  pathological current in LQT3. Nadolol lacks this pharmacological
                  property. Despite less favourable dosing frequency (TID vs. QD),
                  the mechanistic superiority of propranolol over nadolol in LQT3
                  makes it the beta-blocker of choice in this subtype.

  Mexiletine (Class I — ESC 2022 upgrade):
                  The mainstay second agent for LQT3. Blocks INaLate, reducing
                  QTc and preventing pause-dependent TdP. ESC 2022 upgraded
                  mexiletine from Class IIa to Class I for LQT3 with prolonged
                  QTc. Long-term registry (n=34 LQT3, median 36 months):
                  significant reduction in all arrhythmic events.

  Flecainide (variant-level precision):
                  For the p.Ile1768Val-SCN5A variant specifically. This unique
                  variant increases Nav1.5 channel availability via accelerated
                  recovery from inactivation — a distinct biophysical mechanism
                  from the typical INaLate gain-of-function. Flecainide was
                  selected to target this specific channel property. This
                  represents the most granular level of variant-specific
                  prescribing currently in clinical use for LQTS.

  RCSD (right cardiac sympathetic denervation):
                  Used as incremental escalation on top of LCSD + ICD in
                  patients with continued breakthrough events despite multi-drug
                  therapy. Applied in 5 LQT3 patients (3% of the LQT3 cohort).
                  Never deployed as a primary or bilateral-first procedure.

  Cardiac transplantation (last resort):
                  The most extreme therapeutic endpoint — applied in 4 LQT3
                  patients (2.6% of LQT3 cohort) with intractable arrhythmia
                  and multiple ICD shocks refractory to all other measures.
                  Continuous intravenous lidocaine served as a pharmacological
                  bridge to transplant. High-risk variants involved: p.Arg1623Gln-
                  SCN5A, p.Phe1486Leu-SCN5A, p.Leu618Phe-SCN5A (two with
                  LQT3-MEPPC phenotypic overlap). Cardiac transplant eliminates
                  the arrhythmogenic substrate entirely.

  SCN5A gene therapy:
                  ABE8e-SpRY base editing via dual AAV9 corrected SCN5A-M1875T
                  in mice at 54% editing efficiency — normalised QTc and APD90,
                  reduced late INa by 66%; ~20% editing efficiency was sufficient
                  to prevent arrhythmias via electrotonic coupling. Strong
                  preclinical data; no human trials yet.

RARE LQTS SUBTYPES — HIGH-RISK CLINICAL RECOGNITION

  Calmodulinopathy (CALM1/CALM2/CALM3):
                  Impaired Ca²⁺-dependent inactivation of multiple ion channels
                  simultaneously → extreme QTc prolongation with neonatal onset.
                  Any neonate with QTc >600 ms and no other identifiable cause
                  MUST have calmodulin gene sequencing (CALM1/2/3 are all
                  ClinGen-definitive for LQTS). Mortality is very high without
                  aggressive management; standard LQTS therapies are typically
                  insufficient.

  Timothy syndrome (LQT8, CACNA1C GOF):
                  CACNA1C gain-of-function → increased ICaL → extreme QTc
                  prolongation + autism + webbed digits + immune deficiency.
                  Rare but highly malignant; associated with bradycardia-
                  dependent arrhythmia.

  Jervell and Lange-Nielsen (JLN) syndrome:
                  KCNQ1 or KCNE1 homozygous/compound heterozygous — autosomal
                  recessive. Sensorineural deafness + severe LQTS; high
                  mortality without early aggressive management.

INTENTIONAL NONTHERAPY — WHEN ACTIVE MANAGEMENT IS WITHHELD

  18% of the Mayo Clinic LQTS cohort (n=1,304) received intentional nontherapy
  as a deliberate clinical decision. This was NOT neglect — it was precision
  medicine applied to the lowest-risk phenotype.

  Criteria for intentional nontherapy (all must be met):
    - Post-pubertal patient
    - Completely asymptomatic (no syncope, no palpitations, no near-syncope)
    - Normal resting QTc
    - Normal QTc recovery during and after exercise stress testing
    - Phenotypic regression over time or historically mislabelled events

  Active precautionary measures REPLACE pharmacotherapy:
    - QT-prolonging drug avoidance (CredibleMeds list)
    - Electrolyte correction (K⁺, Mg²⁺ optimisation)
    - Fever control and prompt antipyretic treatment
    - Avoidance of extreme dehydration

  Outcomes: Zero lethal events. Zero non-lethal breakthrough cardiac events
  at mean follow-up of 7.5±4.3 years. Intentional nontherapy was used in
  29% of LQT3 patients — the highest proportion of any subtype (reflecting
  that many LQT3 patients have relatively mild phenotypes despite the severe
  subtype reputation, and that post-pubertal LQT3 risk attenuates).

ICD OVERIMPLANTATION IN LQTS — A SYSTEMIC PROBLEM

  119/1,304 patients (9.1%) in the Mayo Clinic specialist cohort carried a
  pre-existing ICD before their first specialist evaluation. After shared-
  decision-making reassessment by LQTS specialists, MORE THAN HALF underwent
  ICD extraction. This illustrates systematic ICD overimplantation at non-
  specialist centres — driven by LQTS diagnosis reflexively triggering device
  implantation without adequate phenotypic risk stratification.

  Clinical implications:
    - Most LQTS patients can be managed successfully WITHOUT an ICD
    - ICD implantation should be based on phenotypic risk alone (1-2-3 LQTS
      Risk Calculator; Class IIa recommendation); never as an automatic
      consequence of a genetic diagnosis
    - ICD to enable sports participation should NOT be performed (Class III)
    - ICD complications (inappropriate shocks, anxiety, PTSD, lead failure)
      represent real harm — the benefit-risk equation must be individualised
    - Overimplantation at non-specialist centres argues strongly for referral
      to cardiogenetic specialist services before device decisions are made


BRUGADA SYNDROME — GENE-SPECIFIC CLINICAL MANAGEMENT
=====================================================

TRIGGER PHYSIOLOGY — THE FOUNDATION OF BrS MANAGEMENT

  Brugada syndrome arrhythmias occur at rest, during sleep, after large meals,
  and during fever — periods of high vagal tone or relative bradycardia. Exercise
  is NOT a trigger. This is the opposite of LQTS and CPVT, and directly determines
  the management approach: sympathetic augmentation is protective, not harmful.

PHARMACOLOGICAL MANAGEMENT

  Quinidine (primary antiarrhythmic):
    Mechanism: Quinidine inhibits transient outward current (Ito) — the primary
    pathological current in BrS repolarisation hypothesis. By blunting Ito, it
    restores the action potential dome in RVOT epicardium and eliminates the
    repolarisation gradient that drives phase 2 re-entry.
    Evidence: Belhassen series (n=60) — quinidine suppressed VF inducibility
    at programmed ventricular stimulation in ~90% of patients.
    Dosing: Low-dose regimen ≤600 mg/day improves tolerability. Side effects
    drive cessation in ~1 in 3 patients; regular full blood count required
    (quinidine-associated haematological toxicity).
    Indications: Recurrent ICD shocks not controlled by ablation; ICD declined
    or contraindicated; co-existing atrial fibrillation (quinidine's combined
    Ito and IKr blockade provides dual utility).
    Evidence gap: The QUIDAM RCT (hydroquinidine vs. placebo) was underpowered
    and failed to reach statistical significance — yet ZERO serious arrhythmic
    events occurred in the hydroquinidine arm. Clinical use is driven by
    mechanistic rationale and observational data, not RCT proof.

  Isoproterenol (IV — electrical storm only):
    Mechanism: Potentiates ICaL, counteracting the Ito-dominant phase 1 AP
    shortening in RVOT epicardium. Restores AP dome and suppresses VF.
    Indication: Acute electrical storm (≥3 separate episodes of VF within
    24 hours) — isoproterenol is the drug of choice. Also indicated for
    short-coupled PVC-triggered VF.

  Phosphodiesterase III inhibitors (cilostazol, milrinone):
    Alternative to isoproterenol for ICaL potentiation in electrical storm
    when IV isoproterenol is not available or tolerated.

ICD SELECTION — THE BENEFIT-RISK CALCULATION

  The benefit-risk equation for ICD in BrS is strikingly unfavourable compared
  to most other arrhythmia indications:

    Annual SCD risk (asymptomatic, spontaneous type 1 ECG): ~0.19%/year
    Annual ICD complication burden:
      - Inappropriate shocks: 3.3%/year
      - Other complications (lead failure, infection, psychological): 4.5%/year
      - TOTAL complication burden: ~7.8%/year

  This means for every life potentially saved by ICD in asymptomatic BrS,
  approximately 40 patients experience ICD complications. Primary prevention
  ICD in asymptomatic BrS without additional high-risk features likely causes
  net harm — the most striking example of ICD overuse in inherited arrhythmia.

  ICD indications (ESC 2022):
    Class I:    Aborted cardiac arrest or documented sustained VT.
    Class IIa:  Cardiogenic syncope + spontaneous or drug-induced type 1 BrS ECG.
    NOT recommended: Asymptomatic BrS alone without additional high-risk
                     features. Shared decision-making essential.

  Device selection — S-ICD vs. transvenous:
    ~15% of BrS patients fail initial S-ICD sensing screening (due to
    T-wave morphology in right precordial leads).
    S-ICD preferred: Young patients without pacing requirement — avoids
    intravascular infection risk and preserves venous access for life.
    Transvenous dual-chamber: Required when sinus node dysfunction, high-degree
    AV block, or atrial arrhythmia discrimination is needed.

CATHETER ABLATION

  Approach: Combined epicardial + endocardial — epicardial substrate
  modification in the anterior RVOT plus endocardial elimination of
  triggering PVCs. SCB (sodium channel blocker) provocation during
  the procedure unmasks additional substrate for mapping.
  Proposed endpoint: Resolution of J-point elevation despite pharmacologic
  SCB provocation.

  Evidence: Pappone series (n=135) — acute ECG normalisation in 100%;
  maintained in 133/135 (98.5%) at median 10-month follow-up. No competing
  RCT data exist.

  ESC 2022 indications (Class IIa): Recurrent ICD shocks refractory to
  pharmacologic management; ICD declined or contraindicated. Ablation in
  asymptomatic BrS: NOT recommended (Class III).

FEVER MANAGEMENT — CLASS I OBLIGATION

  Fever unmasks the BrS phenotype by increasing the temperature-dependent
  inactivation of Nav1.5, further reducing the already-diminished INa in the
  RVOT epicardium. Prompt antipyretic treatment (paracetamol/acetaminophen)
  during ANY febrile illness is Class I management — not optional. Patients
  and families must be explicitly counselled on this. Hospitalization for
  fever with IV antipyretics is appropriate in high-risk BrS patients.

DRUG AVOIDANCE

  The BrS drug avoidance list (www.brugadadrugs.org) is updated continuously
  and covers: sodium channel blockers (ajmaline, flecainide, propafenone,
  procainamide — INCLUDING those used for other indications); psychotropic
  agents (tricyclic antidepressants, first-generation antipsychotics);
  anaesthetic agents (bupivacaine, propofol in high doses); cocaine; cannabis.
  Patients should present this list to all treating clinicians.

SPORTS AND EXERCISE — DIFFERENT FROM OTHER CHANNELOPATHIES

  AHA/ACC 2025 Sports Statement: No data support competitive sports restriction
  in BrS. Competitive sports participation is reasonable after expert assessment
  and management — a meaningful contrast to ARVC (where exercise is a penetrance
  driver) and classic LQTS guidance.
  Rationale: BrS arrhythmias occur during high vagal tone (rest, sleep, post-
  prandial) — NOT during exercise (high sympathetic tone). Exercise is
  physiologically protective, not dangerous, in BrS.
  Mandatory precautions during sport: Avoid heat exhaustion, exercise during
  febrile illness, and significant dehydration. Adequate hydration must be
  prioritised. Prompt antipyresis if fever develops.


CPVT — VARIANT-SPECIFIC CLINICAL MANAGEMENT
============================================

  CPVT is exercise- and emotion-triggered — the diametric opposite of BrS.
  Adrenergic stimulation drives SR calcium overload → diastolic calcium release
  → delayed afterdepolarisations → triggered bidirectional or polymorphic VT.

VARIANT-SPECIFIC BETA-BLOCKER EFFICACY (Chang 2025, n=964 CPVT patients)

  Beta-blockers are the cornerstone first-line therapy for ALL CPVT patients
  (Class I, ESC 2022). However, response varies dramatically by specific variant:

    p.G2337V:  Beta-blockers effective in 10/11 patients (91%) — highly
               responsive; monotherapy often sufficient.
    p.R420W:   Beta-blockers effective in only 5/17 patients (29%) — poorly
               responsive; early escalation to flecainide is the rule.
    p.C2277R:  Beta-blockers effective in only 2/8 patients (25%) — poorly
               responsive; requires combination therapy from the outset.

  ~30% of all CPVT patients require therapy beyond beta-blockers.
  Variant-specific data should prompt early combination therapy planning
  rather than waiting for breakthrough events on inadequate monotherapy.

FLECAINIDE — FIRST ADD-ON THERAPY

  Flecainide added to beta-blockers is the standard escalation for inadequate
  CPVT control. Variant-specific flecainide use rates from the Chang 2025 cohort:
    p.R420W:   7/9 patients received flecainide + BB combination
    p.C2277R:  6/8 patients received flecainide + BB combination
    p.S2246L:  3/4 patients received flecainide + BB combination
    p.G357S:   0/91 patients required flecainide — predominantly responsive
               to BB monotherapy
    p.G2337V:  0/21 patients required flecainide — predominantly responsive

  ESC 2022: Flecainide + BB + ICD after aborted cardiac arrest: Class I
  (upgraded from IIa in 2022). Flecainide as add-on for inadequate BB response:
  standard clinical practice, though not separately Class I outside post-SCA context.

  Mechanism of flecainide in CPVT (contested, clinically effective regardless):
    Three competing mechanistic lines: (1) antiarrhythmic via Na+ channel blockade
    without affecting Ca²⁺ homeostasis; (2) direct RYR2 open-state blockade
    inhibiting diastolic Ca²⁺ sparks; (3) no direct RYR2 effect. The clinical
    efficacy is established; the primary mechanism remains unresolved.

LCSD — SECOND ESCALATION

  Left cardiac sympathetic denervation (Class IIa, ESC 2022 — upgraded from IIb):
  Indicated when flecainide + beta-blockers are ineffective, not tolerated, or
  contraindicated. Surgical removal of the left stellate ganglion and lower
  thoracic sympathetic chain reduces noradrenaline release at the myocardium,
  directly attenuating the adrenergic trigger for diastolic calcium release.
  Particularly important in CPVT where ICD implantation carries unique risk.

ICD IN CPVT — USE WITH CAUTION

  ICD shocks cause catecholamine surges that can directly exacerbate CPVT
  arrhythmia — triggering the very cascade of events the device is attempting
  to terminate (adrenergic-triggered RYR2 Ca²⁺ release → DADs → VT → more
  shocks → more catecholamines). This ICD-proarrhythmia risk is unique to CPVT.
    - ICD after aborted cardiac arrest: Class I (in combination with BB + flecainide)
    - ICD for arrhythmic syncope or bidirectional/PVT despite max BB + flecainide:
      Class IIa (downgraded from Class I in 2022 — reflecting ICD risk awareness)
    - PES for risk stratification: NOT recommended (Class III) in CPVT
    - Subcutaneous ICD: associated with higher complications in CPVT; transvenous
      preferred when ICD is indicated

VARIANT-SPECIFIC ICD IMPLANTATION RATES (Chang 2025)

    p.S2246L:  7/9 patients received ICD — highest-risk variant
    p.R420Q:   13/24 patients received ICD
    p.G357S:   1/94 patients received ICD — predominantly benign clinical course
    p.G2337V:  1/21 patients received ICD — predominantly benign clinical course

  This dramatic per-variant variation in real-world ICD rates confirms that
  CPVT management is NOT a single-pathway algorithm — it is inherently variant-
  specific. The web-based markslab-cpvtdb.org database allows per-variant
  outcome queries with 3D RYR2 structural visualisation.

SPORTS AND EXERCISE IN CPVT — STRATIFIED NOT UNIVERSAL RESTRICTION

  AHA/ACC 2025 represents a major paradigm shift from universal restriction:
    Gene+/phenotype- (no ectopy on burst exercise testing):
      Competitive sports REASONABLE; discuss prophylactic BB ± flecainide.
    Asymptomatic CPVT with exercise-induced ectopy:
      Can CONSIDER sports after BB + flecainide optimisation with stress test
      normalisation. Adequate suppression = ideally no ectopy; bigeminal PVCs
      may be acceptable; couplets or NSVT require continued intensification.
    Previously symptomatic CPVT:
      Requires BB + flecainide + possibly LCSD with normalised stress test.
    Serial burst exercise stress testing 1-2 times/year for all CPVT athletes.
    ICD should NOT be implanted solely for competitive sports participation.


ARRHYTHMOGENIC CARDIOMYOPATHY — GENE-SPECIFIC RISKS
====================================================

  PKP2:   Most common ACM gene (~2/3 of cases); exercise penetrance dose-
          dependent (cumulative lifetime exercise load drives disease expression);
          3 AAV-PKP2 Phase I/II clinical trials underway.
  DSP:    ALVC archetype (left-dominant ACM); female sex is an independent
          risk factor; DSP-specific risk calculator in development; exercise
          restriction is critical.
  LMNA:   Highest malignant ventricular arrhythmia risk of any DCM gene.
          AF and conduction disease (first-degree AVB, bundle branch disease)
          precede DCM by years to decades — making LMNA-positive AF a red flag
          for impending cardiomyopathy. ICD is indicated BEFORE LVEF reaches
          35% in high-risk patients (LMNA-risk-VTA calculator: lmna-risk-vta.fr
          — integrates LMNA variant type, NSVT, male sex, non-missense
          variants, proband status). ESC 2022 gives ICD before LVEF <35% a
          Class IIa recommendation in LMNA with additional risk factors.
  TMEM43: p.Ser358Leu Newfoundland founder mutation; highly malignant; exercise
          is a clear penetrance driver in TMEM43 (like PKP2) — exercise
          restriction is non-negotiable and demonstrably life-saving.
  PLN:    R14del founder mutation (Dutch/Belgian); exercise is NOT a risk factor
          for PLN — a unique exception among ACM genes. Exercise restriction is
          NOT mandated. Requires explicit gene-specific counselling to avoid
          over-restricting patients who bear an otherwise manageable prognosis
          without unnecessary lifestyle limitation.
  FLNC:   Filamin C truncating variants; 82% SCD or sustained VA rate — the
          highest-risk non-LMNA DCM/ACM gene; highly malignant subgroup
          requiring early ICD consideration even at preserved LVEF.


EXERCISE RESTRICTION — GENE-SPECIFIC DECISION FRAMEWORK
========================================================

  ARVC (PKP2, TMEM43, DSP):
          Competitive and high-intensity endurance exercise is Class III
          (Harm) broadly. Exercise is a direct penetrance driver — structural
          disease expression, inflammatory infiltration, and VA risk are all
          augmented by exercise dose. This is one of the most evidence-supported
          gene-specific restrictions in inherited cardiology.

  PLN (R14del):
          Exercise is NOT a risk factor. Exercise restriction is NOT mandated.
          Patients with PLN-R14del disease who are genotype-positive/phenotype-
          negative should not be told to restrict activity on the basis of
          genotype alone.

  LMNA:   Avoid high-intensity endurance exercise but no strict blanket
          restriction — decisions based on LVEF, arrhythmia burden, and
          LMNA-risk-VTA score rather than genotype alone.

  LQTS (AHA/ACC 2025 paradigm shift):
          Competitive sports with shared decision-making is now REASONABLE
          for phenotypic LQTS (QTc ≥480 ms) under expert supervision, with:
            - Non-selective beta-blockers at therapeutic dose
            - Emergency action plan (EAP) in place
            - AED accessible at the sports venue
            - No breakthrough arrhythmias for ≥3 months
          Concealed LQTS (gene+, QTc <460 ms): sports REASONABLE without
          the above requirements.
          Zero deaths in the largest LQTS sports cohort (n=494 athletes).

  CPVT:   Stratified approach (genotype + burst exercise stress test response)
          rather than universal restriction — see CPVT section above.
          Serial monitoring with burst exercise stress testing 1-2 times/year.

  BrS:    No restriction — arrhythmias occur at rest/high vagal tone, not
          during exercise. Active precautions against heat and fever apply.


HCM ARRHYTHMIA RISK — GENOTYPE-SPECIFIC DIMENSIONS
====================================================

  HCM Risk-SCD (5-year model): Integrates maximum wall thickness, LA diameter,
  LV outflow gradient, family history of SCD, unexplained syncope, NSVT
  frequency, and resting heart rate. Guides ICD primary prevention decisions.

  Atrial myopathy in HCM: MYBPC3 and MYH7 variants drive primary LA fibrosis →
  AF substrate that develops independent of haemodynamic burden (i.e., not
  just a consequence of elevated LVEDP — an intrinsic atrial gene expression
  effect). HCM patients with pathogenic sarcomere variants have disproportionate
  atrial fibrotic remodelling compared to genotype-negative HCM.

  Ion channel remodelling precedes hypertrophy: INaL and ICaL electrophysiological
  changes are detectable in HCM cardiomyocytes before the structural (hypertrophic)
  phenotype emerges — creating a window of arrhythmic risk without anatomical
  disease. Genotype-positive/phenotype-negative HCM individuals may carry
  electrophysiological arrhythmic risk that would be missed by imaging alone.

  Mavacamten (cardiac myosin inhibitor): Reduces Ca²⁺ sensitivity of the
  sarcomere (the final common pathway of HCM), alleviating LVOTO and
  potentially reducing the hypertrophic substrate over time. Arrhythmic
  implications of long-term mavacamten use are under active investigation.


EARLY-ONSET AF — CARDIOMYOPATHY GENE PHENOTYPE PATTERNS
========================================================

  Cardiomyopathy gene variants in EOAF signal atrial myopathy — not pure
  electrical disease. Standard antiarrhythmic strategy (rhythm control, rate
  control, ablation) is likely insufficient as monotherapy; addressing the
  underlying myopathy gene pathway is necessary.

  TTNtv-positive EOAF:
          Higher risk of subsequent ventricular cardiomyopathy (cardiomyopathy
          HR 2.98 after AF diagnosis). Longitudinal CMR surveillance is mandatory
          even when initial echocardiogram is normal — TTNtv-related ventricular
          dysfunction can develop after the atrial manifestation.

  LMNA-positive EOAF:
          AF is frequently the first manifestation of LMNA disease. Structural
          cardiomyopathy and progressive conduction disease follow — sometimes
          years later. Aggressive early rhythm management combined with
          longitudinal LV function surveillance is required. Pacemaker and ICD
          need should be anticipated in long-term planning.

  PKP2-positive EOAF:
          Ongoing ARVC surveillance, exercise restriction, and CMR monitoring
          required even if initial echocardiogram is normal — desmosomal
          disease can manifest in atrial rhythm disturbances before overt
          ventricular structural changes develop.

SECTION 6: EMERGING THERAPEUTIC FRONTIERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

6A. AAV-BASED GENE THERAPY
---------------------------

Adeno-associated virus (AAV9) is the dominant platform for cardiac gene therapy:
non-integrating (largely episomal), low immunogenicity, replication-deficient, and
cardiotropic.

PACKAGING CONSTRAINT — THE CRITICAL LIMITATION
  - Standard AAV capacity: ~4.7 kilobases
  - This precludes direct replacement of large genes:
      RYR2: ~15,000 nt — infeasible for direct AAV delivery
      SCN5A: ~6,048 bp — exceeds standard capacity
  - Workarounds: dual-vector trans-splicing, pathway-targeted delivery (MOG1 for
    BrS to rescue Nav1.5 trafficking; CASQ2 for CPVT1 to bypass RYR2 directly)

ARVC — ACTIVE CLINICAL PROGRAMME
  - Three AAV-PKP2 Phase I/II clinical trials currently underway
  - Flecainide eliminates VA in PKP2 cKO mice — possible mechanistic link via direct
    RYR2 channel blockade; clinical trials are evaluating this combination approach

CASQ2 GENE THERAPY FOR CPVT
  - AAV9-CASQ2 replacement abolished adrenergically induced VT in CASQ2 KO mice;
    >85% arrhythmia reduction sustained ≥1 year in knock-in mice
  - CASQ2 overexpression also suppressed CPVT1 (RYR2) model arrhythmias — provides
    a pathway-level bypass when direct RYR2 targeting is infeasible

MOG1 FOR BRUGADA SYNDROME
  - AAV9-MOG1 (Nav1.5 chaperone) enhanced Nav1.5 cell-surface trafficking and
    reversed arrhythmic phenotype in SCN5A-G1746R knock-in mice — targeting Nav1.5
    folding/trafficking rather than the gene itself; bypasses the SCN5A size barrier

BIOLOGICAL PACEMAKER
  - Gene therapy approaches using TBX18 (transcription factor restoring automatic
    cells from ventricular myocytes) or HCN channel delivery are under investigation
  - TBX18 mRNA (naked, unformulated): demonstrated biological pacing in rats and pigs
  - Target application: patients requiring permanent pacing without device implantation


6B. SUPPRESSION-AND-REPLACEMENT (SUPREP) — MUTATION-AGNOSTIC LQTS THERAPY
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

SupRep was developed precisely because KCNQ1 and KCNH2 have hundreds of rare
disease-causing variants — making allele-specific RNAi clinically impractical.

MECHANISM
  - shRNA simultaneously suppresses BOTH wild-type and mutant endogenous transcripts
  - A shRNA-immune engineered cDNA re-introduces functional wild-type protein
  - Co-delivered via AAV9; mutation-agnostic for any variant in the target gene

VALIDATED RESULTS (Animal Models)
  - LQT1 (KCNQ1): normalised QT interval and APD90 in transgenic rabbits; restored
    physiological beta-adrenergic QT shortening; reduced QT dispersion (Bains 2024)
  - LQT2 (KCNH2): normalised QTc from 470 → 414 ms; suppressed EADs and TdP
    inducibility (Bains 2023)
  - SQTS1 (gain-of-function KCNH2): shRNA silencing alone prolonged QTc from 283
    to 333 ms; reduced TdP inducibility 5/7 → 1/8 animals

CRITICAL LIMITATION
  - Dosing balance is essential: excess suppression worsens LQTS; excess replacement
    risks inducing short QT syndrome
  - This therapeutic window has been demonstrated in animals but NEVER established
    in humans — Phase I dose-escalation trials must define safe ratios first


6C. GENE SILENCING — ASO / siRNA / shRNA
-----------------------------------------

Targeting the RNA level rather than DNA offers reversibility and flexibility
for dominant-negative and gain-of-function mutations.

  - ASO (antisense oligonucleotide): RNase H-mediated mRNA degradation; allele-
    specific or transcript-selective; no viral vector required
  - siRNA: RISC-mediated sequence-specific mRNA cleavage; high potency; requires
    delivery vehicle (LNP or conjugate)
  - Allele-specific siRNA for CASQ2-R33Q: ~60% mRNA/protein reduction, prevented
    arrhythmias in preclinical models (Bongianino 2017)
  - CaMKII inhibitory peptide (AIP) via AAV9: mutation-agnostic pathway modulation;
    suppressed Ca2+ dysregulation in CASQ2 KO mice — demonstrates pathway rather
    than gene-level targeting


6D. PRECISION GENE EDITING — CRISPR AND BASE EDITING
------------------------------------------------------

CRISPR-CAS9 IN CARDIAC CONDITIONS
  - First in-vivo RYR2 CRISPR repair (Pan 2023, CPVT): AAV9-SaCas9, 41% efficiency
    in cardiomyocytes; 0/7 treated vs. 7/8 control had arrhythmias; no off-target edits
  - HCM (MYBPC3): CRISPR corrected heterozygous 4 bp deletion in human preimplantation
    embryos with high efficiency (Ma et al. 2017) — preferential HDR repair
  - PRKAG2 cardiac syndrome (WPW + glycogen storage + heart block): AAV9-CRISPR
    single postnatal injection restored cardiac function — paradigm case for gene
    editing over standard device/ablation-based management
  - Mosaicism threshold: >70% phenotypic correction required to restore cardiac
    function in mouse models; heterogeneous editing carries arrhythmia risk from
    electrophysiological heterogeneity between edited and unedited cells

BASE EDITING — TARGETED SINGLE-NUCLEOTIDE CORRECTION
  - Adenine base editor ABE8e-SpRY for LQT3 (Qi 2024): dual AAV9 split delivery;
    corrected SCN5A-M1875T in mice; 54% editing efficiency; normalised QTc and APD90;
    reduced late INa by 66%; only ~20% editing efficiency sufficient to prevent
    arrhythmias (source-sink electrotonic coupling buffers propagation)
  - Advantage over CRISPR: no DNA double-strand breaks; lower off-target indels
  - Limitation: restricted to specific base conversions (A→G with ABE)
  - Prime editing: third-generation approach — precise sequence changes without DSBs
    and without base-type restrictions; promising for point mutations; delivery
    optimization required

GENE EDITING RISK-BENEFIT FRAMEWORK
  - Gene editing is appropriate when existing therapy is insufficient for disease
    burden (CPVT, PRKAG2, HoFH, TTR amyloidosis)
  - Gene editing is inappropriate when highly effective alternatives exist (isolated
    WPW — catheter ablation achieves 90-95% success)


6E. iPSC-DERIVED CARDIOMYOCYTES (iPSC-CMs)
--------------------------------------------

Patient-specific cardiomyocyte models generated from reprogrammed somatic cells
have transformed inherited arrhythmia research:

  - Enable fine-grained genotype-phenotype mapping across ion channel variants
  - Allow head-to-head comparison of variants within the same gene (e.g., KCNQ1,
    KCNH2) in identical cellular backgrounds
  - Used to discover modifier genes: Chai et al. identified a protective KCNK17 GOF
    and an aggravating REM2 variant in a LQT2 family with intrafamilial discordance
    using WES + iPSC-CM electrophysiology + CRISPR correction
  - Serve as preclinical platforms for SupRep, gene silencing, and base editing
    validation before animal model progression
  - NOS1AP mechanism confirmed in iPSC-CMs: validates NOS1AP as a true modifier of
    cardiac repolarisation via NOS1 activity regulation (Dababneh 2025)
  - Limitation: iPSC-CMs have an immature electrophysiological phenotype (IK1 low,
    spontaneous automaticity) — do not fully recapitulate adult ventricular properties


6F. NEUROMODULATION — GENOTYPE-INFORMED ADJUNCT
-------------------------------------------------

Left Cardiac Sympathetic Denervation (LCSD):
  - Thoracoscopic lower stellate ganglion removal; reduces adrenergic drive to heart
  - Class I (ESC 2022) for LQTS: when ICD is contraindicated/declined, OR when
    patient on full therapy with ICD still has multiple shocks or syncope from VA
  - Class IIa (ESC 2022 upgrade) for CPVT: when flecainide + beta-blockers are
    ineffective, not tolerated, or contraindicated
  - Post-LCSD QTc <500 ms predicts success in LQTS; QTc >500 ms persistent = ICD

SECTION 7: CHALLENGES AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

VARIANT RECLASSIFICATION AS A MOVING TARGET
  - >36% of cardiovascular variants reclassify; predominantly downgrading
  - The >50% VUS rate with broad panel testing leaves most tested individuals
    without actionable genetic information
  - No regulatory or IT infrastructure mandates systematic family notification when
    reclassification occurs — a critical health system gap
  - Re-evaluation every 2-3 years is Class I for HCM and recommended for all
    inherited cardiac conditions; this requires dedicated cardiogenetic centre support

PENETRANCE AND ENVIRONMENTAL MODIFIERS
  - Exercise, medications, pregnancy, fever, alcohol, and illicit drugs all interact
    with genetic substrate to determine clinical expression
  - TTNtv + alcohol: gene-environment interaction increasing DCM/EOAF risk
  - Drug-induced LQTS: ~30% of TdP cases harbour LQTS gene variants — pharmacogenomic
    pre-prescription screening is not yet standard but may become so
  - Polygenic modifier background modulates monogenic variant penetrance by >3-fold
    (TTNtv AF penetrance: 6.7% vs. 21.5% by PRS tertile)

EARLY-ONSET AF AS UNDERRECOGNIZED GENETIC TESTING INDICATION
  - Current guidelines lag: ESC has no recommendation; ACC/AHA only Class IIb
  - EOAF genetic yield (~10%) equals non-familial DCM (Class IB ESC indication) —
    no biological justification for the guideline discrepancy
  - No prospective RCT has demonstrated that genetic testing in EOAF improves outcomes
    vs. standard AF management — the key evidence gap
  - No standardised definition of EOAF: age cutoffs of <30, <40, <45, <60, and <66
    are all used across studies — international consensus definition is needed
  - No data on whether EOAF patients with cardiomyopathy P/LP variants have different
    catheter ablation success rates vs. genotype-negative EOAF patients
  - Stroke in CHA2DS2-VA = 0 young AF (4.1%): current anticoagulation guidelines
    provide no guidance for this high-risk subgroup

NON-CODING RNA IN CARDIAC ELECTROPHYSIOLOGY
  - Long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs): ANRIL, MIAT, H19, MALAT1 all implicated in
    cardiovascular disease loci; regulatory roles in fibrosis and ion channel
    expression are under active investigation
  - LncRNA-MIAT: elevated in cardiac hypertrophy; potential therapeutic target
  - Therapeutic RNA: non-coding RNA-based approaches under preclinical investigation
    for BrS and DCM

PHARMACOGENOMICS IN ARRHYTHMIA MANAGEMENT
  - CYP2D6 and CYP3A4 variants affect metabolism of flecainide, propafenone,
    mexiletine, quinidine — drug-specific metaboliser phenotype may determine both
    efficacy and toxicity
  - HF pharmacogenomics (ADRB1, GRK5, GNB3): beta-blocker response varies by genotype
  - Drug-induced LQTS PRS (OR 1.34/SD): identifies patients at genomic risk of QT
    prolongation during QT-prolonging drug exposure — potential pre-prescription
    screening tool in high-risk prescribing scenarios

HEALTH EQUITY IN GENETIC MEDICINE
  - Most cardiac GWAS and pathogenic variant databases are derived from European-
    ancestry cohorts
  - Variant interpretation in non-European populations is substantially harder;
    higher VUS rates create health equity disparities
  - Multi-ancestry GWAS are being developed (DCM and BrS PRS show early cross-ancestry
    portability) but remain an exception rather than the norm

SECTION 8: CONCLUSION — TOWARD GENOTYPE-GUIDED ARRHYTHMIA CARE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The genetic and genomic revolution in arrhythmia management is no longer emerging —
it has arrived. Key clinical integration milestones already achieved:

  1. DIAGNOSTIC:  Multigene panels provide actionable results in 14-40% of
     arrhythmia/cardiomyopathy patients; combined panels outperform disease-specific
     approaches by identifying cross-phenotype overlap

  2. RISK STRATIFICATION:  Gene-specific calculators (LMNA-risk-VTA, HCM Risk-SCD,
     DSP risk calculator) and GWAS-derived PRS are now complementary layers on top
     of traditional clinical markers

  3. FAMILY PREVENTION:  Cascade screening identifies asymptomatic relatives before
     a sentinel event; genotype-negative relatives can be safely discharged from
     surveillance — a profound clinical efficiency gain

  4. TREATMENT PERSONALISATION:  Subtype-specific therapy is established practice —
     mexiletine for LQT3, flecainide for CPVT, gene-specific ICD thresholds for LMNA
     and PLN; SupRep and CRISPR-based therapies are in the pipeline

  5. NOVEL DISEASE RECOGNITION:  EOAF is now recognised as an arrhythmia-first
     presentation of cardiomyopathy genotype; AF in young patients warrants genetic
     evaluation, longitudinal CMR surveillance, and cascade family screening

Key unresolved challenges for the next decade:
  - Defining actionable PRS thresholds for clinical intervention
  - Establishing prospective outcome data for genetic testing in EOAF
  - Developing standardised variant reclassification notification infrastructure
  - Validating gene therapy safety in humans (SupRep, base editing, AAV-CPVT)
  - Closing the health equity gap in non-European populations
  - International consensus definition of early-onset AF for genetic testing

The transition from treating arrhythmias with drugs and devices alone to correcting
the underlying genetic defect is underway. The molecular era of arrhythmia management
has begun.
