# KCNH2 (hERG)

## Details
KCNH2 (also known as hERG — human ether-à-go-go-related gene) encodes the Kv11.1 α-subunit of the rapid delayed rectifier potassium channel (IKr). Located on chromosome 7q35-q36, it is one of the three primary channelopathy genes and is involved in both LQTS and SQTS.

## Key Facts
- **LQT2 (loss-of-function, autosomal dominant):** Reduced IKr during phase 3 → delayed repolarization → prolonged QT. Accounts for 25–30% of LQTS — the second most common subtype. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- Variants affecting the transmembrane pore of KCNH2 carry the greatest relative risk of cardiac events compared with non-pore transmembrane domain variants — a clinically important genotype-risk stratification finding. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- Lumacaftor was shown to rescue the LQT2 phenotype in iPSC-CMs and is in a phase II clinical trial (NCT04581408) for QTc shortening in LQT2 patients. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- Mexiletine has recently been shown to reduce QTc in LQT2 patients specifically. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **SQTS1 (gain-of-function):** Accelerated IKr repolarization → shortened QT. Quinidine is first-line pharmacotherapy; amiodarone and sotalol are ineffective despite being IKr blockers, likely due to altered drug-channel interactions from the gain-of-function mutation. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- KCNH2 gain-of-function is also implicated in BrS (Ito overlap, rare) and ERS (missense variant p.V392I). ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- IKr is inhibited by estrogen — the primary mechanism explaining higher LQT2/TdP risk in women of reproductive age. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **SupRep for LQT2:** AAV9-SupRep normalized QTc (470 → 414 ms), APD90 (519 → 424 ms), restored β-adrenergic APD shortening, suppressed EADs and TdP inducibility in KCNH2-mutant rabbits (Bains 2023). ([[sources/gene-therapy-arrhythmia-2025]])
- **SQTS1 silencing:** shRNA-only (no replacement) targeting gain-of-function KCNH2-N588K prolonged QTc from 283 → 333 ms and reduced TdP inducibility from 5/7 to 1/8 animals in transgenic rabbits. ([[sources/gene-therapy-arrhythmia-2025]])
- **Dominant-negative KCNH2 for AF:** Adenoviral delivery of KCNH2-G628S in porcine models prolonged atrial APD and reduced AF relative risk to 0.44 (Amit 2010). Effect transient due to adenoviral expression loss at ~21 days. ([[sources/gene-therapy-arrhythmia-2025]])

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- **Bidirectional KCNH2 phenotype — opposite mutations requiring opposite treatments:** KCNH2 loss-of-function causes LQT2 (managed by avoiding QT-prolonging drugs, beta-blockers), while gain-of-function causes SQTS1 (managed by quinidine to prolong QT). A patient with a novel KCNH2 variant of uncertain significance cannot be safely stratified without functional characterization — the variant's direction of effect determines whether the patient needs QT prolongation or shortening therapy. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **SQTS1: quinidine first-line despite amiodarone/sotalol being IKr blockers:** In SQTS1, both amiodarone and sotalol are IKr blockers and would theoretically lengthen QT, yet they are ineffective, likely because the gain-of-function mutation alters drug-channel binding kinetics. This counter-intuitive pharmacology creates prescribing confusion — clinicians familiar with LQTS may reflexively prescribe sotalol or amiodarone for a patient with SQTS1 arrhythmias, with potentially ineffective results. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **Dominant-negative KCNH2 for AF — transient effect without clinical viability:** Adenoviral delivery of dominant-negative KCNH2-G628S prolonged atrial APD and reduced AF inducibility in porcine models, but the therapeutic effect was transient (~21 days) due to adenoviral expression loss. This approach has not been developed further for clinical translation; the current gene therapy evidence for KCNH2-based AF therapy is preclinical and transient. ([[sources/gene-therapy-arrhythmia-2025]])
