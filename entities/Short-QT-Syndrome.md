# Short QT Syndrome (SQTS)

## Details
Short QT syndrome is a rare autosomal dominant channelopathy characterized by a QTc interval <340 ms due to accelerated cardiac repolarization. It predisposes patients to atrial fibrillation, ventricular fibrillation, and sudden cardiac death.

## Key Facts
- Prevalence: 0.02–0.1% of adults; male predominance; low penetrance. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **8 subtypes:** SQT1–3 caused by gain-of-function mutations in K⁺ channel genes (KCNH2, KCNQ1, KCNJ2); SQT4–6 by loss-of-function in Ca²⁺ channel subunits (CACNA1C, CACNB2, CACNA2D1); SQT7 by SCN5A R689H loss-of-function; SQT8 by SLC4A3 loss-of-function affecting Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchange. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **ClinGen-curated core genes (4 genes):** KCNH2 (definitive), KCNQ1 (moderate), KCNJ2 (moderate), SLC4A3 (moderate). SLC4A3 (cardiac chloride-bicarbonate exchanger AE3): loss-of-function causes reduced membrane localization → intracellular alkalinisation → shortened cardiomyocyte AP duration. Two families identified with the identical SLC4A3 variant. ([[sources/arrhythmia-genetics-mgenetik-2025]])
- **~30% of SQTS patients have SCA/SCD as the first clinical manifestation.** Life-threatening arrhythmias equally common across genotypes. Syncope more frequent in males; atrial arrhythmias (flutter, AF, palpitations) distributed equally. ([[sources/arrhythmia-genetics-mgenetik-2025]])
- **Phenocopies and exogenous causes to exclude:** congenital lactase deficiency, primary carnitine deficiency (SLC22A5 gene), primary hyperparathyroidism, Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY), acidosis, digitalis intoxication, hyperthermia, hyperkalemia, hypercalcemia, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, hyperthyroidism, vitamin A intoxication, milk-alkali syndrome, rufinamide (anti-convulsant), ATP potassium channel openers (pinacidil, levcromakalim). ([[sources/arrhythmia-genetics-mgenetik-2025]])
- **Variant detection rate:** ~20–25% in index patients. An indicative pathogenic variant (ACMG class 4/5) scores 2 points in the Gollob score and is an important diagnostic feature. ([[sources/arrhythmia-genetics-mgenetik-2025]])
- Belongs to the spectrum of early repolarization syndrome. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **Diagnosis:** QTc shortening plus ≥1 of: survived VF/VT without structural disease; confirmed pathogenic mutation; family history of SQTS. QTc ≤320 ms alone is sufficient; QTc 320–360 ms requires additional arrhythmic syncope history. Schwartz Score used for probability stratification. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **Management:** ICD is primary therapy for symptomatic patients at SCD risk with life expectancy >30 years. Quinidine is first-line pharmacotherapy; hydroquinidine can prolong QT via IKr blockade. Amiodarone and sotalol are ineffective in SQTS1 despite being IKr blockers — likely due to mutation-altered drug-channel interactions. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- Pharmacological data is limited, primarily focused on SQTS1; genotype-targeted drug research is needed. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])

- **ESC 2022 guideline recommendations — revised diagnosis criteria:**
  - SQTS diagnosed with QTc ≤360 ms **plus ≥1** of: (a) pathogenic mutation, (b) family history of SQTS, (c) VT/VF survival without structural heart disease: **Class I**. (More inclusive than the prior QTc <340 ms threshold.) ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - QTc ≤320 ms alone: consider SQTS **Class IIa**. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - QTc 320–360 ms + arrhythmic syncope: consider SQTS **Class IIa**. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - Genetic testing: **Class I**. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - ICD for SCA survivors or documented sustained VT: **Class I**. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - ILR in young SQTS patients: **Class IIa**. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - Quinidine: **Class IIb** if ICD contraindicated/refused, or in asymptomatic patients with family history of SCD. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - Isoproterenol for electrical storm: **Class IIb**. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])
  - PES for SCD risk stratification: **NOT recommended (Class III)**. ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]])

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- **Amiodarone/sotalol paradox in SQTS1:** Despite both being IKr blockers, amiodarone and sotalol are clinically ineffective in SQTS1 — the primary pathogenesis of which is gain-of-function IKr. The explanation is that the gain-of-function KCNH2 mutation alters drug-channel binding kinetics such that normal IKr-blocking drugs cannot exert their effect. This mechanistic exception undermines the assumption that channel-blocking drugs reliably reverse channel gain-of-function, a lesson relevant to other channelopathies. ([[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
- **SupRep for SQTS1 — silencing without replacement:** Gene therapy for SQTS1 requires only shRNA silencing (suppression) of the gain-of-function KCNH2 allele — no replacement needed. However, this is opposite to the SupRep approach for LQT2 (where replacement is needed after silencing). Over-silencing of KCNH2 in a SQTS patient risks causing LQT2. The therapeutic window for "silencing only" must be narrow and has not been validated in humans. ([[sources/gene-therapy-arrhythmia-2025]])
- **ESC 2022 diagnostic threshold expansion:** ESC 2022 expanded SQTS diagnosis to QTc ≤360 ms with additional criteria (vs. prior QTc <340 ms). This broader definition captures more patients but may reduce diagnostic specificity — many healthy individuals with borderline short QTc will now meet criteria with even a single additional feature (e.g., a family history of SQTS that could itself be undiagnosed). ([[sources/VA-SCD-ESC-2022]], [[sources/channelopathies-jaha-2025]])
